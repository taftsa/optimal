optimal is an attempt to use crowdsourced experience to make the act of selecting ensemble literature simpler. The goal is to help us all pick the optimal piece in an efficient way.

The platform seeks to go beyond traditional mono-dimensional grade designations in order to allow you to find the perfect piece for your ensemble. Rather than a single number to tell you how hard it is, each piece in the optimal database gets 6 numbers, each corresponding to a specific area of difficulty.

Anyone who teaches music can analyze music for optimal. When you look at a piece's rating, you're seeing the average rating from everyone who has rated it. This means you are seeing the professional consensus rather than the opinion of one person you've never met.

Create an ensemble once you're familiar with the rating system by clicking "New Band," "New Choir," or "New Orchestra" and following the Google Forms prompts. If you create multiple ensembles with the same name under the same email address, the newest one should override older ones.

If you forget what you named you ensemble, simply enter your email address into the email address field, then click the Forgot [ensemble] Name button. This will show you popups (JavaScript alerts) with the name of each band associated with that address.

Search lets you search by title, composer, and/or arranger. Make sure to check the appropriate box(es).

List Optimal shows you all pieces that match your ensemble's skill profile perfectly.

List Performable shows you all pieces that have difficulty profiles within your ensemble's abilities.

List Challenging shows you pieces that will stretch your students by one level in one to six of the nodes. The "Challenge Level" is the number of nodes that will stretch your ensemble's abilites.

List All shows you every piece in the database.

Submit Analysis takes you to a Google Form that allows you to submit your analysis of a piece.

Click the ? button near the instrumentation/voicing graph to toggle help boxes.

Hover over the ? in the corner of a piece's card to see information about who has analyzed it.

Filters can be used to narrow your search.

  • The state filter shows you only pieces that have been analyzed for optimal by someone in the state you selected.
  • The grade filter shows you only pieces that are reported to have been assigned a specific grade by a state organization.
  • The classification filter shows you only pieces that fit into certain categories like "march" and "overture" according to the analyzer(s).
  • The language filter (choir only) shows you only pieces that are in (in whole or in part) the selected language.

As long as you are using a modern browser, it will remember what filters you are using until you close it (whole browser, not just the tab).

Access these filters by clicking "Filters" in the top menu. Select your filters, then click "Apply." Filters use the equivalent of the AND boolean, so selecting a grade of "5" and a classification of "Latin" will show you only pieces that are both Latin in style and grade 5.

Filters are a separate layer from Search, Optimal, Performable, Challenge, and All. For example, if you want to find filtered challenging pieces, first click "List Challenging," then apply filters.

If you decide not to filter your results or want to remove filters, leave everything on "Any" and click "Apply." This will show you everything in the view you are currently in.

Please note that the filters do not communicate with each other, so selecting a state of "Virginia" and grade of "5" will show you pieces that have been graded in Virginia and are a grade 5 somewhere, but that doesn't mean those pieces are all grade 5 in Virginia

Rapidity refers to the speed with which the muscles involved in music-making need to move in order to perform the music. It is related to, but not synonymous with, tempo. It principally refers to motion of the fingers, hand, tongue, and vocal mechanism. It does not refer to rapid dynamic changes.

Rhythm refers to the complexity of the rhythms, meters, and articulation patterns present in the piece. This includes individual rhythms as well as the relationship between rhythms between groups. It can also include peculiarities in the way a rhythm is written, even if it is easier to perform than it looks. It does not refer to the quantity or variety of simultaneous rhythms.

Dynamics refers to volumes and changes in volume initiated by the individual performers. Soft volumes are of particular concern. The frequency and range of dynamic changes are to be considered. It does not refer to changes in overall group volume caused by changes in texture.

Texture refers to the number and nature of simultaneous, distinct musical lines. Texture difficulty typically arises from a large quantity of distinct lines or exposure from thin scoring. Related concepts include transparency and uniformity.

Tonality refers to the key(s) (or lack thereof) and amount of chromaticism used in the work. The difficulty arises from the use of less-familiar keys, notes, and motion between notes. Care must be taken to distinguish difficulty related to tonality from difficulty related to rapidity. For band instruments, a fast run in the key of B♭ has mostly rapidity difficulty, while the same run in B♮ has both rapidity and tonality difficulty.

Range refers to the highness or lowness of pitches that must be played or sung relative to the typical ranges of the instruments or voices. For bands, primary concerns are trumpet and clarinet, with flute and all other brass as secondary concerns.

Concepts such as "expression," "aesthetic," and "style" are not included. These are not elements of difficulty, but rather are the result of the mastery of the identified six elements and their interactions. They are important, but are not fundamental in determining whether a piece is performable.

The optimal platform utilizes a standard concert band consisting of parts rather than players, as not every player can cover a part alone.

  • 1 Flute (F)
  • No Oboe (O)
  • No Bassoon (OO)
  • 2 Clarinets (C)
  • 3 Saxophones: Alto, Tenor, and Baritone (S)
  • 2 Trumpets (T)
  • 1 F Horn (H)
  • 1 Trombone (R)
  • 1 Baritone (B)
  • 1 Tuba (U)
  • 1-5 Percussion (P)

Instrumentation as compared to this standard is assessed as part of the analysis process and is reflected below the difficulty graph.

  • If an instrument is not needed, its box is empty.
  • Standard instrumentation (using the abbreviations above) is highlighted in light blue and is listed on the top row.
  • Expanded instrumentation (abbreviations with a + except for O and OO) is highlighted in maroon and is on the bottom row.
  • The average minimum number of percussionists is indicated below the "P," with the "P" highlighted in the appropriate color.

The optimal platform shows the relative difficulty of the five standard string orchestra parts. Any additional parts are noted below the graphs.

  • If there is no part for a given instrument, the box will display with no background color.
  • If the part for a given instrument is easier than what would be expected given the difficulty graph, the box will display in green.
  • If the part for a given instrument is comparable to what would be expected given the difficulty graph, the box will display in gold.
  • If the part for a given instrument is harder than what would be expected given the difficulty graph, the box will display in maroon.

The optimal platform shows the number of Soprano, Alto, Tenor, and Bass parts in a piece, as well as whether or not piano is needed. Any other instrumentation is noted separately below the graphs.

  • If there are no parts needed for a given voice type, the box will display with no background color.
  • If there is one part needed for a given voice type, the box will display in green.
  • If there are two parts needed for a given voice type, the box will display in pink.
  • If there are three parts needed for a given voice type, the box will display in maroon.
  • If there are four or more parts needed for a given voice type, the box will display in black.
  • Piano (P) with show without background for no piano, pink for optional piano, and maroon for required piano.
  • The aggregate voicing (SSA, SATB, TTB, etc.) is displayed in the box below the SATB boxes

Only analyze music you've taught; the value of optimal is in the opinions of those who have had practical experience with the music.

Use standards of "This piece's ___________ has the difficulty of a grade ___________ piece" and "This ensemble performs ___________ to the level of a grade ___________ piece." When grading marches, no comparison with "other marches" should be made. This will mean that the ratings of tonality, texture, and likely dynamics will typically be lower than the other nodes for marches.

After you analyze a piece, you will have to refresh the page in order to see it.

Pieces in the optimal are graded on a scale of 1-6, as are pieces on many state lists. While this is a highly subjective grading system, the following is a general guide for how to apply these grades:

  • Grade 1 pieces and elements are performable by the average beginning ensemble in its second semester.
  • Grade 2 pieces and elements are performable by the average second-year ensemble in its second semester.
  • Grade 3 pieces and elements are performable by the average third-year ensemble in its second semester.
  • Grade 4 pieces and elements are performable by the finest middle school ensembles and the average high school ensemble.
  • Grade 5 pieces and elements are performable by better-than-average high school ensembles.
  • Grade 6 pieces and elements are performable by the finest high school ensembles and average college ensembles.
